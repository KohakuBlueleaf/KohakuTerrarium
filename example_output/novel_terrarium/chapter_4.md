"I am calling the authorities, Clara," Higgins’s voice sounded muffled, as if submerged under heavy water. "You are entirely ruined. Do you hear me?"

Clara didn't hear him. Her entire universe had contracted to the circumference of the black plastic earpiece pressed against her skull. The horrific sounds of the train wreck—the groaning steel, the roaring fire—were beginning to ebb, replaced by a creeping, cavernous echo. 

"Clara?" Elias murmured. His voice was no more than a fragile wisp of breath. "Are you crying, sweetheart? Please don't cry."

"I'm not," she lied, but a wet sob betrayed her.

"It's so cold here," Elias whispered. The wet rattle in his chest was gone, replaced by a terrifying, hollow stillness. "And dark. I can't see the windows anymore. Why hasn't anyone come for us?"

Clara squeezed her eyes shut. She was keeping him here. Her desperate, selfish grief was anchoring his soul to the twisted wreckage of the train. As long as she pretended he was alive, he was trapped in this agonizing purgatory, bleeding out in the dark. To truly love him meant letting him go.

She took a deep, shuddering breath. "Elias, listen to me."

"I'm listening, darling."

"You don't need to worry about the debts," she said, her voice dropping to a tender, unwavering pitch. "You don't need to worry about the rent, or the house in Oak Park, or any of it. I forgive you. For all of it."

"Forgive me?" Confusion rippled through the static. "Clara, what are you talking about? I'm coming home."

"You're already home, my love," she whispered, hot tears flowing freely down her face, cutting through the ash and grime of the night shift. "You fought so hard. But it’s okay to stop now. You can close your eyes. You can rest."

Silence stretched across the impossible distance. The Ethereal Frequency hummed, a delicate vibration against her ear. 

"Oh," Elias breathed softly. There was no fear in that single syllable. Only a quiet, blossoming understanding. "I see."

"I love you," Clara said fiercely. "I will always love you."

"I love you too, Clara. So much." His voice was fading, dissolving into the ether like smoke in the wind. "The blue hydrangeas..."

The harsh, tearing static suddenly smoothed out into a low, peaceful hum, like a tuning fork struck in an empty room. Then, with a soft *click*, the line went completely dead. The sickly amber light on the switchboard flickered once, twice, and extinguished into a dull, tarnished black.

Clara sat in the silence for a long moment. Behind her, the rhythmic clack of the switchboard had tentatively resumed. Higgins stood a few feet away, his mouth open, though whatever final threat he had prepared died in his throat as he looked at her face. 

Above them, the first gray light of dawn began to filter through the grimy, high windows of the basement exchange, cutting through the smell of ozone, sweat, and hot copper. 

Connect, disconnect. 

Clara gripped the brass jack. With a steady hand, she pulled the plug from the dead board and let the cord drop. Her debts remained, her job was gone, and a vast, terrifying grief awaited her. But as she stood from her stool and turned to face the morning light, the crushing weight of the dark was finally gone.
